A trading fee is the most direct revenue lever you have as a market
operator. It's also the easiest one to misprice. Set it too high and you
choke volume; set it too low and you leave real money on the table — money
your audience would have happily paid.

This is a practical guide to picking the right number.

## What range actually works

<Callout type="tip" title="Rule of thumb">
  Most successful Kuest operators sit between **0.8% and 2.5%** in their
  first 6 months. Below 0.8% you're competing with Polymarket on price you
  can't sustain; above 2.5% you start losing the bottom of the order book.
</Callout>

The sweet spot depends on your audience type:

- **Crypto-native communities** — comfortable with 1.5–2.5% fees because
  they expect on-chain costs. Bots will avoid you above 2%.
- **Mainstream sports / pop culture** — 1–1.8% is the cap before retention
  collapses. Casual traders compare fees to free sportsbook bets, not other
  on-chain venues.
- **Institutional / professional** — 0.5–1% fees, but with much larger
  ticket sizes. The volume math beats the per-trade margin.

## The three fee mistakes we see most often

1. **Pricing for revenue, not for volume.** A 3% fee on $10k weekly volume
   yields $300. A 1% fee on $200k weekly volume yields $2,000. The lower
   fee is almost always the right call early.
2. **Changing fees too often.** Traders price your fee into their behavior.
   Two changes in a month destroys trust. Pick a rate and hold it for at
   least 90 days.
3. **Forgetting maker/taker dynamics.** When you can, give makers a 0.2–0.4%
   discount. Their resting orders are what makes your book look alive.

## How to evolve the fee as you grow

As your venue scales past $1M monthly volume, you have room to introduce
tiers — institutional accounts at lower fees, retail at standard, bots
metered separately. But that complexity is overhead. Don't add it until
you're past the cold-start phase and the simpler model is leaving real
money on the table.
